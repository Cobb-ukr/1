Fillings

What they are:

A restorative treatment used to repair a tooth that has been damaged by decay. The decayed part is removed, and the cavity is filled with materials such as composite resin, amalgam, or porcelain.

Why they're needed:

Fillings restore tooth structure, prevent further decay, and improve the tooth's strength and function. They are also aesthetic when composite resins are used to match the natural color of the teeth.

Types of Dental Fillings:

  • Composite Resin: Tooth-colored fillings made of plastic and fine glass particles. Ideal for visible areas as they can be matched to your natural tooth color.
  • Amalgam: Silver-colored fillings made from a mixture of metals including silver, tin, copper, and mercury. Known for their durability and strength, especially for back teeth.
  • Gold: Gold foil or cast gold restorations that are extremely durable but more expensive.
  • Porcelain/Ceramic: Custom-made in a lab, these match tooth color and are resistant to staining.
  • Glass Ionomer: Made from acrylic and glass, these release fluoride to help protect teeth and are often used for fillings below the gum line.

The Filling Procedure:

  1. Examination: The dentist examines the tooth, often using X-rays to determine the extent of decay.
  2. Anesthesia: Local anesthesia is administered to numb the area around the affected tooth.
  3. Decay Removal: The dentist removes the decayed portion of the tooth using a drill, laser, or air abrasion instrument.
  4. Cleaning: The cavity is cleaned of bacteria and debris.
  5. Filling Placement: If the decay was near the root, a liner may be put down to protect the nerve. The filling material is then applied in layers.
  6. Finishing: The filling is shaped to match your bite, and any excess material is trimmed off. The filling is then polished.

Benefits of Different Filling Materials:

Material Benefits Best for
Composite Resin Natural appearance, bonds to tooth structure Front teeth, visible areas
Amalgam Durable, less expensive, long-lasting Back teeth, high-pressure areas
Gold Extremely durable, non-corrosive Partial crowns, back teeth
Porcelain Stain-resistant, natural appearance Larger restorations, inlays/onlays
Glass Ionomer Releases fluoride, good for children Non-biting surfaces, root surfaces

After Getting a Filling:

  • You may experience some sensitivity to hot and cold for a few days or weeks
  • Avoid eating or drinking until the numbness wears off
  • Follow your dentist's instructions for care
  • If you feel pain when biting or a sharp edge, contact your dentist for an adjustment

Lifespan of Fillings:

The durability of fillings varies by material and oral care habits:

  • Amalgam: 10-15 years
  • Composite Resin: 5-10 years
  • Gold: 15-30 years
  • Porcelain: 10-15 years
  • Glass Ionomer: 5 years

Preventing the Need for Fillings:

  • Brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste
  • Floss daily
  • Limit sugary foods and drinks
  • Visit your dentist regularly for check-ups and cleanings
  • Consider dental sealants for cavity-prone areas
